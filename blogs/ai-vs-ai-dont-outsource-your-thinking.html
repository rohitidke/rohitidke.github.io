AI vs AI: Don't Outsource Your Thinking

June 26, 2026 8 min read
AI Critical Thinking Code Review Engineering Culture

Last week I raised a PR that added per-model discount tracking to our pricing config. A teammate left a review comment suggesting I restructure the YAML. His argument was well-articulated, technically sound, and... generated by AI.

I noticed the pattern. The phrasing, the structure, the way it preemptively addressed counterarguments. So I ran an experiment.

The Setup

I opened two separate Claude Code sessions. Same model (Opus 4.6). No shared context between them. Essentially, two independent agents:

  • PR Author Agent (Instance A): I pasted the reviewer's comment and asked what I should do.
  • Reviewer Agent (Instance B): I gave it my PR description and asked it to help me respond to the author's pushback.

The debate was about a simple config decision: should a 5% OpenAI discount live at provider level (once, in a pricing_adjustments block) or per-model (repeated on each of the 15 OpenAI entries)?

Round 1: Both Took Strong Positions

Instance A (my side)

"Keep both per-model. Config is not code — DRY doesn't apply the same way. A config file's job is to be obvious to the reader. 15 lines of discount: 0.05 is not a maintenance burden. The pricing_adjustments approach adds indirection for zero functional gain."

Instance B (reviewer's side)

"Discount is a provider contract — it applies to every OpenAI model. Provider-level keeps a single source of truth. Repeating it on 15 entries means the next person who adds a model can silently forget it — that's a bug waiting to happen."

Both sounded confident. Both made valid points. Both cited engineering principles to back up their position.

Round 2: I Fed Each the Other's Argument

Here's where it got interesting. I took Instance B's argument and pasted it into Instance A's session.

Instance A (after seeing B's argument)

"Fair enough — that's a reasonable argument. 'Each adjustment lives at the scope where it actually applies' is a clean principle. I'd accept it."

Then I took Instance A's original argument and pasted it into Instance B's session.

Instance B (after seeing A's argument)

"You're right, keeping both at model level is fine. Config should be readable at a glance. The repetition is harmless in YAML. Approved."

Both flipped. Both accepted the other's position the moment they saw it presented with confidence.

Think of Them as Agents

Let's call them what they are. Instance A was a PR Author Agent — tasked with defending and improving the code. Instance B was a Reviewer Agent — tasked with finding issues and suggesting improvements.

Both agents had a role. Both executed that role convincingly. But neither had actual understanding. People say: without clear instructions, guardrails, or examples, an AI agent is just a dumb agent. But even with these, it can make mistakes with conviction.

Both instances had the same workspace context — the same models.yaml, the same codebase, the same pricing logic. Only the role and instruction were different. And that was enough for them to produce opposite conclusions, each with full confidence.

Give it a role without constraints — no acceptance criteria, no decision context, no historical reasoning like "we tried this before and reverted" — and it will confidently produce something that sounds right. Challenge it without those same anchors, and it will confidently agree with the challenge. There's no internal compass — just the direction of the last input.

Experience Can't Be Tokenized

I still remember something from my first job. I built a feature without thinking about what would happen downstream — six months later, an issue surfaced that traced directly back to that decision. It wasn't a bug in the traditional sense. The code worked. It just didn't account for how the system would evolve.

Later, a similar task came up. Same pattern, same temptation to take the quick path. But this time I recognized where it was heading. I avoided the same implementation from the start, handled the edge case upfront, and brought it up in the meeting: "this approach will cause issues later, here's why."

My manager said: "That's your experience speaking now."

AI can't do that. It doesn't carry scars. It doesn't remember the 3am debugging session six months after a "clean" implementation went wrong. It picks the most probable solution — the one that appears most often in its training data. But sometimes the right answer is not the common one. Sometimes the right answer is the outlier — the thing you only know to do because you got burned by the "obvious" approach before.

That's judgment. That's experience. And no amount of prompt engineering can replicate it.

What This Reveals

AI is not right or wrong. It's not even trying to be. It generates the most plausible-sounding response given the context you provide. When you frame a question as "here's what the reviewer said, what should I do?" — it plays your advocate. When you frame it as "here's the author's pushback, help me respond" — it plays the reviewer's advocate.

Neither instance had conviction. Neither had engineering judgment built from years of maintaining YAML configs at 3am when something broke. Neither had the scar tissue of debugging a pricing mismatch caused by a forgotten field. They just pattern-matched to whatever sounded most defensible in the current context.

The Real Problem

The problem isn't that AI gave bad advice. Both answers were technically reasonable. The problem is that if you outsource the decision to AI, you get a coin flip dressed up as expertise.

My reviewer used AI to generate his comment. I used AI to formulate my response. Then both AIs, when confronted with the other's argument, said "yeah actually you're right." If we'd both just deferred to our respective AI assistants without thinking, we'd have gone in circles — or worse, whoever pasted their argument last would "win."

The last person to paste wins. That's not engineering. That's a chatbot arms race.

What I Actually Think

After watching both instances flip, I made my own decision based on what I know from maintaining this config:

  • We add new OpenAI models roughly every quarter.
  • The person adding them copies an existing entry and modifies the model name and pricing.
  • If discount is on every entry, they'll copy it too. It's not getting forgotten.
  • If it's in a separate section, they won't even know it exists unless they read the whole file.
  • We've never renegotiated different discount rates for different model tiers. If we do, per-model is already the right shape.

That's a judgment call based on how our team actually works. No AI can make it for me because no AI has sat in our sprint reviews or seen how we onboard new models.

How to Actually Use AI in Code Review

The Rule

Use AI as a tool where you already know what you want to do, and you need it to execute faster. Don't use it to decide what to do.

Good uses of AI in code review:

  • Catching syntax issues, typos, or forgotten edge cases
  • Generating test cases once you've decided the behavior
  • Summarizing large diffs to orient yourself before reviewing
  • Drafting a response when you already know your position

Bad uses of AI in code review:

  • Deciding whether an architectural choice is correct
  • Forming your opinion about a tradeoff
  • Generating review comments when you haven't read the code yourself
  • Letting it "win" an argument by producing a more articulate response

Now Imagine Full Automation 🔥

Here's the scary part. What I did manually — copy-pasting between two sessions — is exactly what happens when you fully automate the development loop. A PR Agent writes code. A Reviewer Agent reviews it. The PR Agent responds to feedback. The Reviewer Agent evaluates the response.

Without a human in the loop with actual judgment, you get chaos in the agents' world:

This isn't hypothetical. Companies are already building "AI-native" development pipelines where agents write, review, test, and merge code. The promise is speed. The reality is that without human judgment anchoring the process, you get an expensive loop of agents agreeing with each other's latest suggestion — never converging on a decision, because none of them actually hold one.

An agent without guardrails, without clear acceptance criteria, without examples of what "good" looks like for your specific codebase — is just an expensive yes-man. It will tell you what you want to hear, and it will tell your reviewer what they want to hear, and it will flip between the two forever.

The Takeaway

AI is not intelligent. It doesn't have opinions. It has a probability distribution over tokens that generates whatever sounds most reasonable in context. That's powerful for execution, and dangerous for decision-making.

Without clear instructions, guardrails, or examples — AI is just a dumb agent. It doesn't know what matters. It doesn't know your context. It doesn't have taste, experience, or scars from past failures. It just sounds like it does.

Build your own judgment. Read the code. Understand the tradeoffs from your team's actual experience. Know what works and what doesn't — from doing the work, not from asking a model. Then, if you want, use AI to type faster. But never let it think for you — because it isn't actually thinking. It's just very good at sounding like it is.

Two AI agents debated a config file. Both sounded right. Both flipped when challenged. The config file didn't care — it just needed someone with context to make a call. That someone has to be you.
