Life Is More Fun When You Treat It Like a Game

April 8, 2026 6 min read
Mindset Personal Growth Games Life
This is one of the simplest mindset shifts that has helped me enjoy life more: treat life like a game, treat yourself like the character, and focus on playing well instead of obsessing over every result.

I realized that life becomes a lot more fun when I look at it the way I look at a game. In a game, you are a character. You have some goals. You have missions to complete. Some tasks move the main story forward, and some are just side quests.

Once I started seeing life this way, things became clearer. Instead of feeling lost or emotionally tangled in every situation, I could simply ask: what is the mission right now, and what is the next move?

You Are the Character, but You Are Also the Player

This idea helped me create a small distance from my thoughts and emotions. It felt like I was controlling my body the same way I control a character with a joystick, except here I have much more control.

My mind still talks. It distracts me. It says many things. But if I am the player, then the mind is not the boss. It is just one part of the experience. I do not have to obey every thought. I can still do what is needed.

The moment I remember I am playing, I stop panicking so much about every little scene.

Main Story Missions First, Side Quests Later

Games teach this naturally. First, you do the main story mission. Then, if you want, you go do side missions. In real life, I think this works beautifully too.

Main story missions

  • Your health
  • Your work
  • Your long-term goals
  • Your relationships
  • The responsibilities that truly matter

Side missions

  • Watching TV
  • Scrolling aimlessly
  • Random entertainment
  • Anything that feels good but does not move your real story forward

The point is not to remove fun. The point is to know what deserves priority. When the main mission is clear, life feels less messy.

Mission Failed? Try Again

In a game, if you fail a mission, you do not sit there crying for the next ten hours. You restart. You learn something. You try again. Failure feels lighter because it is part of the process.

Life can be approached the same way. If one attempt does not work, it does not have to become a full identity crisis. Maybe the mission failed. Fine. Retry. Adjust. Move.

This mindset reduces attachment to results. You still care, but you do not collapse just because one outcome did not go your way.

Observation Creates Presence

One beautiful side effect of this mindset is that it puts me in the present moment. If I keep observing myself, almost like watching the character from a third-person view, I become more aware of what I am doing right now.

Sometimes I literally laugh at myself. I notice my reactions, my habits, my drama, and I think: okay, this is the role I am playing right now. That small distance makes things lighter.

When you observe yourself often, you stop getting dragged around by the mind so easily. You become more present, and even small moments start feeling good.

Games Also Teach Fearlessness

In games, you usually do not overthink every move forever. You try. You fight. You explore. You do what you are capable of doing. There is a natural fearlessness in that.

Real life becomes more powerful when we bring some of that same energy into it. Not recklessness, but willingness. Less paralysis. More action.

My God of War Reminder

I noticed this clearly while playing God of War. There are Valkyrie fights that are extremely challenging. Freeing them is difficult, and that is exactly what makes it fun.

I enjoyed those fights even when they were hard. I did not win just because I wanted to win. I kept playing because the challenge itself was enjoyable.

I could not beat the final Valkyrie. And honestly, that was okay. I did not get stuck in sadness because I failed to complete all of them. I simply moved on to the next game, the next goal, the next challenge.

Real life gives us the same freedom. There are many goals we can choose from. Many battles. Many challenges. We do not have to chase every single one. We can choose the ones that feel meaningful and fun.

Choose the Game You Want to Play

That may be the most important part. In life, unlike many games, you often have the freedom to choose your direction. So choose the goals that give you energy. Choose the challenges that make you feel alive. Choose the missions that you would actually enjoy trying to complete.

Winning is great. Losing is also fine. What matters most is whether you are truly playing.

Final Thought

Life feels lighter when I stop treating every moment like a permanent judgment and start treating it like gameplay. Do the mission. Stay aware. Retry when needed. Do not get too attached. Enjoy the process.

Maybe that is what life is: just play, have fun!
